Ways to say "Whereas" — with examples

  • Whereas Some prefer cities, whereas others enjoy the countryside.→ mid-sentence, formal comparison
  • While / Whilst While technology has benefits, it also has drawbacks.→ start or mid-sentence, slightly less formal
  • In contrast City life is fast-paced. In contrast, rural life is quiet.→ start of new sentence, followed by comma
  • By contrast The first group improved. By contrast, the second did not.→ same as "in contrast"
  • Unlike Unlike her sister, she is very outgoing.→ Unlike + noun, start of sentence
  • As opposed to He chose quality as opposed to quantity.→ comparing two options directly
  • On the other hand It's expensive. On the other hand, it's very reliable.→ presenting the other side
  • Conversely Rich countries consume more. Conversely, poor countries produce less waste.→ formal, opposite situation

Not only … but also — How to build it

  • Mid-sentence She is not only smart but also hardworking.→ no inversion, natural order
  • Start of sentence Not only does she speak French, but she also speaks German.→ INVERSION: Not only + auxiliary + subject
  • Past Not only did he pass the exam, but he also got the highest score.→ Not only + did + subject + base verb
  • Present perf. Not only has she finished, but she has also helped others.→ Not only + has + subject + past participle
  • With modal Not only can he sing, but he can also play guitar.→ Not only + modal + subject + base verb

Conditionals (B2 must-know)

  • 2nd If I had more time, I would study abroad.→ hypothetical present/future
  • 3rd If I had worked harder, I would have passed.→ regret / past hypothetical
  • Mixed If I had studied more, I would be fluent now.→ past cause, present result

Passive Voice

  • Present It is widely believed that…
  • Past The law was introduced in 2010.
  • Future Steps will be taken to address this.
  • Modal The issue could be solved by…
Great for formal writing – avoids saying "I/we"

Perfect Tenses

  • Present Perf. I have lived here for 5 years.
  • Pres. Perf. Cont. I've been studying for hours.
  • Past Perfect By the time I arrived, she had left.

Modal Verbs

  • Certainty must / can't (deduction)
  • Possibility might / could / may
  • Advice should / ought to
  • Obligation must / have to / need to
  • Past modal should have, could have, might have

Relative Clauses

  • Defining The city that I live in is quite noisy.→ no commas – identifies which
  • Non-defining London, which is the capital, is very diverse.→ commas – adds extra info

Reported Speech

  • She said (that) she was tired.
  • He told me he would call later.
  • She asked where I lived.
  • He suggested going by train.
  • She admitted having made a mistake.

Contrasting Structures

  • Although + clause: Although it is expensive, it is worth it.
  • Despite + noun/-ing: Despite being tired, she worked.
  • Even though + clause (stronger emphasis)
  • In spite of + noun/-ing

Gradable Adjectives

  • Gradable very / quite / rather + cold, good, tired
  • Non-gradable absolutely / completely + freezing, perfect, exhausted
  • Boosters incredibly, remarkably, extraordinarily

Future Forms

  • will → prediction / spontaneous decision
  • going to → plans / evidence-based prediction
  • Pres. Continuous → fixed arrangement
  • Future Continuous I'll be working at 8pm.
  • Future Perfect I'll have finished by then.
  • likely to / bound to → degrees of certainty

Used to / Would

  • used to + inf. → past states & habits: I used to play football.
  • would + inf. → repeated past actions: We would visit every summer.
  • be used to + -ing → accustomed: I'm used to waking up early.

Inversion (formal / essay)

  • Not only Not only does she work hard, but she also volunteers.→ aux + subject after negative opener
  • Hardly Hardly had I arrived when it started raining.
  • No sooner No sooner had she left than the phone rang.
  • Seldom Seldom do we see such talent.
  • Never Never have I seen anything like it.
Rule: negative adverb + auxiliary + subject + verb

Cleft Sentences (emphasis)

  • It-cleft It is education that changes lives.→ It is/was + focus + that/who
  • What-cleft What we need is more investment.→ What + clause + is + focus
  • All-cleft All I want is a fair chance.
  • The reason The reason why I moved is that I got a new job.
Great for Writing Task 2 to emphasise key points

Wish / If only

  • Present regret I wish I lived closer to work.→ wish + past simple = "now"
  • Past regret I wish I had studied harder.→ wish + past perfect = "before"
  • Annoyance I wish he would stop interrupting.→ wish + would = complaint about others
  • If only = stronger version of "I wish" (same grammar)

Articles — Tricky Cases

  • No article ∅ Education is important. ∅ Poverty is rising.→ general/abstract uncountable nouns
  • The The education system in France…→ specific/defined
  • No article ∅ People often think… (NOT "The people")→ people in general
  • The The people I met were kind.→ specific people
  • A/An There has been a significant increase…→ singular countable first mention

Gerund vs Infinitive

  • + -ing enjoy, avoid, consider, suggest, mind, risk, deny, admit→ I enjoy reading. She avoids eating late.
  • + to inf. want, need, decide, plan, hope, expect, manage, afford→ I decided to leave. She hopes to pass.
  • Both (diff. meaning) stop, remember, try, forget, regret
  • I stopped smoking = quit ≠ I stopped to smoke = paused in order to smoke
  • I remember locking the door = memory of past ≠ Remember to lock = don't forget

Word Formation (Reading key skill)

  • Noun → Adj benefit → beneficial, economy → economic/economical
  • Verb → Noun develop → development, achieve → achievement
  • Adj → Adv significant → significantly, considerable → considerably
  • Negatives un-, in-, im-, ir-, dis-, mis-→ unavoidable, inaccurate, impossible, irregular, disadvantage, misleading
  • Suffixes -tion, -ment, -ness, -ity, -ful, -less, -able, -ive

Adverbs of Frequency

  • 100% always, constantly, invariably
  • ~80% usually, normally, generally, typically
  • ~60% often, frequently, regularly
  • ~40% sometimes, occasionally, from time to time
  • ~15% rarely, seldom, hardly ever
  • 0% never, under no circumstances
Position: before main verb (She often reads) / after "be" (He is usually late)

Adverbs of Degree

  • Very strong extremely, incredibly, remarkably, exceptionally
  • Strong highly, greatly, significantly, substantially
  • Moderate fairly, quite, rather, reasonably, moderately
  • Weak slightly, somewhat, a little, marginally
  • Almost not barely, hardly, scarcely→ She barely passed. I hardly slept. (= almost didn't)
barely/hardly/scarcely = negative meaning → don't use with "not"!

Uncountable Nouns (TRAP!)

  • NEVER plural information, advice, furniture, equipment, luggage
  • NEVER plural research, evidence, knowledge, news, progress
  • NEVER plural homework, behaviour, traffic, accommodation, feedback
  • NEVER plural money, work, weather, transport, vocabulary
  • How to count them a piece of advice / information / furniture / evidence
  • How to count them an item of luggage / equipment / clothing
  • Singular verb! The news is good. (NOT "are")
❌ informations ❌ advices ❌ furnitures ❌ equipments ❌ knowledges ❌ homeworks

Subject-Verb Agreement (TRAP!)

  • Everyone / Nobody Everyone is responsible. Nobody knows.→ always singular, even though it feels plural
  • Each / Every Each student has a book. Every person needs help.→ always singular
  • Neither…nor Neither the teacher nor the students were happy.→ verb agrees with the NEAREST subject
  • The number of The number of students is rising.→ singular (= the total)
  • A number of A number of students are absent.→ plural (= several)
  • Collective nouns The government is/are… (both OK in British English)

Common Preposition Traps

  • ❌ depend of → ✅ depend on
  • ❌ interested for → ✅ interested in
  • ❌ good in → ✅ good at
  • ❌ married with → ✅ married to
  • ❌ arrive to → ✅ arrive at (a place) / arrive in (a city/country)
  • ❌ listen ∅ → ✅ listen to
  • ❌ discuss about → ✅ discuss ∅ (no preposition!)
  • ❌ explain me → ✅ explain to me

So / Such / Too / Enough

  • so + adjective It was so cold (that) we stayed inside.
  • such + (a) noun It was such a cold day (that) we stayed inside.→ such a + adj + singular noun
  • too + adjective It's too expensive to buy. (= excessively)→ too = negative, don't use for positive ("too beautiful" is wrong unless it's a problem)
  • adjective + enough She's old enough to drive.→ enough comes AFTER the adjective
  • enough + noun We don't have enough time.→ enough comes BEFORE the noun

Make vs Do vs Take vs Get

  • MAKE a decision, a mistake, progress, an effort, a suggestion, money, a complaint, sure
  • DO homework, research, business, damage, your best, an exam, a favour, exercise
  • TAKE a decision (also OK), a break, an exam, notes, responsibility, action, place, into account
  • GET a job, married, worse/better, used to, ready, rid of, in touch with
❌ make homework ❌ do a mistake ❌ make an exam

Other Classic B2 Traps

  • ❌ "I am agree" → ✅ "I agree" (agree is a verb, not adjective)
  • ❌ "It depends of" → ✅ "It depends on"
  • ❌ "I am born in 1995" → ✅ "I was born in 1995"
  • ❌ "The most of people" → ✅ "Most people"
  • ❌ "I went to home" → ✅ "I went home" (no preposition with home)
  • ❌ "She said me" → ✅ "She told me" / "She said to me"
  • ❌ "According to me" → ✅ "In my opinion" ("according to" = citing someone else)
  • ❌ "I look forward to hear" → ✅ "I look forward to hearing" ("to" is a preposition here → + -ing)

Confusing Word Pairs (Reading traps)

  • affect (verb) vs effect (noun)→ Pollution affects health. It has an effect on…
  • economic (= of the economy) vs economical (= cheap/efficient)
  • rise (no object) vs raise (+ object)→ Prices rise. They raise prices.
  • lay (+ object) vs lie (no object)
  • principal (= main / head) vs principle (= rule / belief)
  • its (possessive) vs it's (= it is)

Speaking – Key Strategies

  • Never give one-word answers — always expand with a reason or example.
  • Use Part 1 to warm up; in Part 3, show analytical thinking.
  • Link your ideas: because of this / which means that / as a result
  • Use a mix of tenses: past habits, present situations, future plans.
  • Don't self-correct too much — fluency matters more than perfection.
  • Use fillers naturally: well, you know, I mean, actually

Writing – Task 1 (Letter)

  • Identify the tone: formal / semi-formal / informal — and stick to it.
  • Address ALL bullet points in the task — missing one drops your score.
  • Minimum 150 words. Aim for 160–180.
  • Formal = no contractions (it's → it is).
  • Structure: Opening → Point 1 → Point 2 → Point 3 → Closing.

Writing – Task 2 (Essay)

  • Minimum 250 words. Aim for 260–290.
  • Plan 3–4 minutes before writing.
  • Use 4 paragraphs: Intro / Body 1 / Body 2 / Conclusion.
  • Each body paragraph = 1 main idea + explanation + example.
  • Avoid repeating the same word — use synonyms and pronouns.

Grammar Mix for Band 6

  • Use at least one conditional (2nd or 3rd) per essay.
  • Use passive voice in formal writing.
  • Use relative clauses to combine sentences.
  • Vary sentence length — mix short punchy sentences with longer complex ones.
  • Use modal verbs to soften claims: could, might, may

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting every sentence with "I" in the essay.
  • Overusing "also" — use furthermore / moreover / additionally
  • Writing "in my opinion I think" → pick one.
  • Using very informal language in a formal letter.
  • Copying words from the task question — paraphrase them.

Time Management

  • Writing: Task 1 = 20 min | Task 2 = 40 min
  • Speaking Part 1: 4–5 min | Part 2: 2 min talk | Part 3: 4–5 min
  • In Part 2: use 1 min to take notes, then speak for the full 2 minutes.
  • In Task 2: leave 3–4 min to proofread at the end.
Band 6 Golden Rule: You don't need to be perfect — you need to be clear, consistent, and varied. Use a range of grammar structures, choose precise vocabulary, and always support your ideas with a reason or example. That's what gets you to 6.0!
